    75→    75→        <div class="idea-title" style="font-size:16px">1. Score the client, not just the contractor</div>
    76→    76→        <div class="idea-sub">The most contrarian move in the market — and the one nobody has made</div>
    77→    77→      </div>
    78→    78→      <div style="display:flex;align-items:center;gap:8px">
    79→    79→        <span class="badge b-unique">Most unique</span>

    83→    83→    <div class="idea-body open">
    84→    84→      <p><strong>The insight:</strong> Contractors currently have zero information about clients before signing a contract. They don't know if a client pays late, issues endless variation orders, disputes legitimate claims, or abandons a project halfway. They're flying completely blind. No platform in Kenya — or East Africa — scores clients.</p>
    85→    85→      <p><strong>What it looks like:</strong> Every procuring entity (developer, NGO, county government) gets a Client Profile. Contractors rate clients after project completion on: payment timeliness, scope creep frequency, site accessibility, fairness in dispute resolution, and variation order handling. Clients get a score. Clients with low scores pay a premium to attract contractors — or simply can't attract good ones at all.</p>
    86→    86→      <p><strong>Why it's defensible:</strong> The moment contractors start checking client scores before bidding, you have supply-side lock-in. Contractors now have a reason to use the platform actively — not just react to being rated. You've turned a passive rating system into an active marketplace where both sides have skin in the game.</p>
    87→    87→      <p><strong>The negotiating leverage this creates:</strong> A county government with a reputation score of 42 (known to delay payments by 6 months) needs your platform to improve that score before good contractors will bid on their tenders. You become a reputational infrastructure provider that both sides depend on — not just a directory one side uses to check on the other.</p>
    88→    88→      <p><strong>Revenue angle:</strong> Organisations pay for "Client Profile" verification and can purchase "Reputation Boost" advisory (consulting on what's driving their low score and how to fix it). KES 50,000–200,000/engagement. New revenue stream from a completely different buyer.</p>

    95→    95→        <div class="idea-title" style="font-size:16px">2. Milestone escrow — eliminate the #1 reason projects fail</div>
    96→    96→        <div class="idea-sub">M-Pesa-native payment escrow tied to platform milestone confirmations</div>
    97→    97→      </div>
    98→    98→      <div style="display:flex;align-items:center;gap:8px">
    99→    99→        <span class="badge b-unique">Most impactful</span>

   103→   103→    <div class="idea-body">
   104→   104→      <p><strong>The problem it solves:</strong> The two biggest fears in Kenyan construction are (a) contractor abandons site with advance payment, and (b) client refuses to release payments after work is done. These fears drive up costs, kill small contractors, and make clients risk-averse. No platform addresses the underlying payment trust problem.</p>
   105→   105→      <p><strong>What it looks like:</strong> Client deposits the full contract value into a Wajenzi-managed M-Pesa escrow account at contract signing. As each milestone is confirmed by both parties on the platform, the corresponding payment tranche is released automatically to the contractor. No milestone confirmed = no payment released = no contractor abandonment risk. No disputed work = no payment withheld risk.</p>
   106→   106→      <p><strong>Why M-Pesa makes this uniquely possible in Kenya:</strong> Escrow in the US requires banks, lawyers, and weeks. In Kenya, M-Pesa's B2C API means you can move money instantly with a programmatic instruction. The infrastructure to do this already exists. Nobody has applied it to construction.</p>
   107→   107→      <p><strong>The moat:</strong> Once a client has used escrow for one project and slept well, they will never go back to informal payment arrangements. The switching cost is the peace of mind. You become the trust layer for Kenyan construction finance.</p>
   108→   108→      <p><strong>Revenue:</strong> 1–2% escrow fee on funds held. A KES 10M project held in escrow for 12 months at 1.5% = KES 150,000 from one project. At 20 projects/month that's KES 3M/month from escrow fees alone — entirely passive once the system is built. You also earn float on the held funds if structured correctly with a banking partner.</p>
   109→   109→      <p><strong>Partnership needed:</strong> A Kenyan commercial bank (NCBA, Equity, KCB) as escrow holder. You are the platform, they hold the money. Their compliance infrastructure + your platform intelligence = a genuinely new product neither could build alone.</p>

These clients are in London, the US, Canada, the Gulf. They cannot visit the site. They cannot check on the contractor. They hire based on family recommendations and then watch their money disappear. This is one of the most underserved, highest-pain, highest-spending segments in East Africa.</p>
   126→   126→      <p><strong>What the diaspora client needs that nobody provides:</strong> A contractor they can trust without being physically present. Verifiable credentials. Progress photos with timestamps and GPS tags. A platform that holds payment in escrow and releases it only on confirmed milestones. The ability to video-call the site from abroad. All of this in one product.</p>
   127→   127→      <p><strong>Why Wajenzi is perfectly positioned:</strong> You already have everything except the diaspora-specific UI: verified contractors, milestone tracking, M-Pesa escrow, and document verification. You need to add GPS-tagged photo uploads, a diaspora client portal with timezone-aware notifications, and international payment rails (Stripe/Wise for GBP, USD, AED inflow).</p>
   128→   128→      <p><strong>Distribution:</strong> Diaspora communities are intensely networked. One satisfied client in London tells 20 people in their community group. Target Kenyan church networks, WhatsApp investment groups, and YouTube channels that cater to diaspora audiences. The customer acquisition cost per diaspora client is low; their willingness to pay is very high.</p>
   129→   129→      <p><strong>Revenue:</strong> Premium tier at KES 30,000–80,000/project for diaspora clients (vs KES 2,000 for a local search). Escrow fee on larger international transfers. Potential to build the "Wajenzi Diaspora" sub-brand as a distinct product.</p>
